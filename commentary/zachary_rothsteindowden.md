As Thomson argues, the metaphor is for words as fletched arrows that fly straight to their mark. With this image can be contrasted the adjective ἁμαρτοεπής 'whose words miss the mark' of [Il. 13.824](http://homer.beta.newalexandria.info/read/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0012.tlg001.perseus-grc2:13.821-14.13). The same conception of words as missiles is implicit in the use of ἵημι in the sense of 'utter' (LSJ I.A.2, cf. [Il. 3.221-2](http://homer.beta.newalexandria.info/read/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0012.tlg001.perseus-grc2:3.211-3.240)).



Thomson, J. A. K. “[Winged Words](https://www.jstor.org/stable/637139).” *The Classical Quarterly*, vol. 30, no. 1, 1936, pp. 1–3.

This scene is depicted on an Athenian black-figure vase dating to the mid 6th century, now at the Louvre (E869, BAPD [310146](http://www.beazley.ox.ac.uk/record/AE95183E-9069-4C20-AC8A-9472CFE22DE0)). Achilles receives a wreath and a shield from Thetis. Both are labeled. To his right stands Odysseys, facing away. His name is spelled ΟΛΥΤΕΥΣ as is usual in non-litterary Attic. The variant with Δ appears supplant the variant with Λ in the second half of the 5th century.



Kretschmer, Paul. *[Die griechischen Vaseninschriften ihrer Sprache nach untersucht](https://www.google.com/books/edition/Die_griechischen_Vaseninschriften_ihrer/0ggWAAAAYAAJ?hl=en&amp;gbpv=0)*. Gütersloh, Bertelsmann, 1894, pp. 146-148.


Brommer, Frank. “[Zur Schreibweise des Namens Odysseus](https://www.jstor.org/stable/40848691).” *Zeitschrift Für Vergleichende Sprachforschung*, vol. 96, no. 1, 1982, pp. 88–92.
